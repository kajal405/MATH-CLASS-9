1 · Sets

Definition • Representation • Types • Operations • Laws • Cardinality • Venn diagrams • Examples • Quiz

1.1 Definition

A set is a well-defined collection of distinct objects considered as an object in its own right. The objects are called elements or members of the set.

Sets are usually denoted by capital letters: A, B, C. Elements are written in braces: A = {1,2,3}.

1.2 Examples

  • Finite set: {1,2,3,4}
  • Set of vowels: {a,e,i,o,u}
  • Set-builder: {x | x is an even number < 10} = {0,2,4,6,8}
  • Empty (null) set: ∅ or {}

1.3 Methods of Representation

  1. Roster (tabular) form: list elements inside curly braces. Example: A = {1, 3, 5}.
  2. Set-builder form: specify a property that elements satisfy. Example: B = { x | x is an even integer less than 10 }.
  3. Venn diagrams: circles showing relationships among sets — used for visualizing unions, intersections, and complements.

1.5 Operations on Sets

  • Union (A ∪ B): elements belonging to A or B or both. Example: {1,2} ∪ {2,3} = {1,2,3}.
  • Intersection (A ∩ B): elements common to both A and B. Example: {1,2} ∩ {2,3} = {2}.
  • Difference (A \ B): elements in A not in B. Example: {1,2,3} \ {2} = {1,3}.
  • Complement (A'): elements in universal set U not in A. If U = {1..5} and A = {1,2}, then A' = {3,4,5}.

1.6 Laws of Set Algebra (with formulas)

  • Commutative laws: A ∪ B = B ∪ A, A ∩ B = B ∩ A.
  • Associative laws: (A ∪ B) ∪ C = A ∪ (B ∪ C) and similar for ∩.
  • Distributive laws: A ∩ (B ∪ C) = (A ∩ B) ∪ (A ∩ C); A ∪ (B ∩ C) = (A ∪ B) ∩ (A ∪ C).
  • Identity laws: A ∪ ∅ = A, A ∩ U = A.
  • Complement laws: A ∪ A' = U, A ∩ A' = ∅.
  • De Morgan's laws: (A ∪ B)' = A' ∩ B', (A ∩ B)' = A' ∪ B'.
  • Idempotent laws: A ∪ A = A, A ∩ A = A.

1.7 Cardinality

The cardinality of a finite set A, denoted |A|, is the number of elements in A. Example: |{2,4,6}| = 3.

1.8 Worked Examples

  1. Let U = {1,..,10}, A={1,2,3,4}, B={3,4,5}. Compute A∪B, A∩B, A\B, B\A and |A|, |B|. — Solution: A∪B={1,2,3,4,5}; A∩B={3,4}; A\B={1,2}; B\A={5}; |A|=4; |B|=3.
  2. If A={1,3,5}, B={2,4,6}, show A∩B=∅ and A∪B={1,2,3,4,5,6}.

2 · Taxation

Definition • Importance • Types • Principles • Short Q&A • Formula • Quiz

2.1 Definition

Taxation is the system by which a government collects money from individuals and organizations to fund public services, infrastructure and administration.

2.2 Importance of Taxation

  • Funds public goods: schools, hospitals, roads.
  • Redistributes income through progressive tax systems and subsidies.
  • Regulates economic activity (e.g., sin taxes to discourage unhealthy consumption).

2.3 Types of Taxes

Taxes are broadly classified into direct and indirect taxes:

TaxDirect / IndirectExample
Income taxDirectTax on personal income
Corporate taxDirectTax on company profits
GST / VATIndirectTax on goods & services
Excise dutyIndirectOn fuel, tobacco, liquor

2.4 Principles of Taxation

  • Equity: fairness in distribution of tax burden.
  • Certainty: taxpayers should know how much is due and when.
  • Convenience: payment should be convenient for payer.
  • Economy: cost of collection should be minimal.

2.5 Short Questions & Answers

Q: What is a direct tax?
A: A tax paid directly to the government by the person on whom it is imposed (e.g., income tax).

2.6 Simple Formula

Tax Payable = Taxable Income × Tax Rate

2.7 Worked Example

If taxable income is ₹250,000 and tax rate is 10%, tax payable = ₹25,000.

Definition • Angles • Triangles • Congruency • Similarity • Quadrilaterals • Circle • Formulas • Coordinate Geometry • Quiz

3.1 Definition & Basic Concepts

Geometry is the branch of mathematics dealing with shapes, sizes, relative positions of figures, and properties of space.

Basic objects: point, line, segment, ray, angle, plane.

3.2 Types of Angles (with measures and examples)

3.3 Angle-pair relationships (meaning & example)

  • Complementary: two angles summing to 90°. Example: 30° and 60°.
  • Supplementary: two angles summing to 180°. Example: 110° and 70°.
  • Vertically opposite angles: formed by two intersecting lines; opposite angles are equal.
  • Linear pair: adjacent angles that add to 180°.

3.4 Triangles

Classification by sides: equilateral (all sides equal), isosceles (two equal sides), scalene (no equal sides).

Classification by angles: acute, right, obtuse.

Properties
  • Sum of interior angles = 180°.
  • An exterior angle = sum of the two opposite interior angles.
Congruent triangles — concept & rules

Two triangles are congruent if they have identical shape and size. Congruency rules: SSS, SAS, ASA, RHS (for right triangles).

Similar triangles — concept & rules

Triangles are similar if their corresponding angles are equal and corresponding sides are in proportion. Rules: AA, SAS, SSS similarity.

3.5 Quadrilaterals (6 types) & properties

  1. Square: all sides equal, all angles 90°.
  2. Rectangle: opposite sides equal, angles 90°.
  3. Parallelogram: opposite sides parallel and equal; opposite angles equal.
  4. Rhombus: all sides equal; diagonals bisect each other at right angles.
  5. Trapezium (trapezoid): one pair of parallel sides.
  6. Kite: two pairs of adjacent equal sides; one pair of opposite equal angles.

3.6 Circle — important terms & formulas

Key terms: center, radius (r), diameter (d = 2r), chord, arc, sector.

Formulas: area = πr², circumference = 2πr, length of arc = (θ/360) × 2πr when θ in degrees, area of sector = (θ/360) × πr².

3.8 Pythagoras theorem

In a right triangle with legs a, b and hypotenuse c, Pythagoras' theorem states: a² + b² = c². Worked example: if a=3 and b=4, c=5.

3.9 Coordinate Geometry basics (distance, midpoint, section)

  • Distance formula between (x₁,y₁) and (x₂,y₂): √((x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)²).
  • Midpoint: ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2).
  • Section formula (internal division of line joining (x₁,y₁) and (x₂,y₂) in ratio m:n): ((mx₂ + nx₁)/(m+n), (my₂ + ny₁)/(m+n)).

4 · Real Numbers

Rational & Irrational • Decimal expansions • Surds • Examples • Quiz

4.1 Rational and Irrational Numbers

Rational numbers are numbers that can be expressed as p/q where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0. Examples: −2, 0.75 (3/4), 1/3.

Irrational numbers cannot be written as a ratio of two integers. Examples: √2, π, √3.

4.2 Decimal Expansions

Rational numbers have decimal expansions that either terminate (e.g., 0.25) or repeat periodically (e.g., 0.333...). Irrational numbers have non-terminating, non-repeating decimals.

4.3 Surds

A surd is an expression containing an irrational root, for example √3. Rules for simplifying surds and rationalizing denominators are covered below.

Worked Examples & Properties

  1. Show that √2 is irrational (classic proof by contradiction using parity).
  2. Convert 1/8 to decimal: 0.125.
  3. Express 0.333... as a fraction: 1/3.

5 · Algebra

Expressions • Identities • Factorisation • Methods • Quiz

5.1 Definition & Basic Terms

Algebra uses symbols (usually letters) to represent numbers in expressions and equations. Terms: variable, coefficient, constant, monomial, polynomial.

5.2 Algebraic expressions

Example: 3x² + 5x − 2. Here 3 is coefficient of x², x is variable, −2 is constant.

5.3 Types of expressions

  • Monomial (one term): 5x³
  • Binomial (two terms): x + 3
  • Trinomial (three terms): x² + 5x + 6
  • Polynomial: sum of monomials

5.4 Algebraic identities (10 important)

  1. (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
  2. (a − b)² = a² − 2ab + b²
  3. a² − b² = (a − b)(a + b)
  4. (a + b + c)² = a² + b² + c² + 2ab + 2bc + 2ca
  5. (x + y)³ = x³ + 3x²y + 3xy² + y³
  6. (a − b)³ = a³ − 3a²b + 3ab² − b³
  7. a³ + b³ = (a + b)(a² − ab + b²)
  8. a³ − b³ = (a − b)(a² + ab + b²)
  9. (a + b)(a − b) = a² − b²
  10. Use these identities to expand and factorise quickly

5.5 Factorisation — methods

  • Common factor extraction: ax + ay = a(x + y)
  • Factor by grouping: split polynomial into groups with common factors
  • Use identities: difference of squares, perfect square trinomials, sum/difference of cubes

Worked example: Factorise x³ − y³

Use formula: (x − y)(x² + xy + y²).

7 · Trigonometry

Definitions • Ratios • Standard values • Table • Quiz

7.1 Basic concept (right triangle)

Trigonometry deals with relationships between angles and sides of triangles. For a right triangle with angle θ:

  • sin θ = opposite / hypotenuse
  • cos θ = adjacent / hypotenuse
  • tan θ = opposite / adjacent
  • Reciprocals: cosec θ = 1/sin θ, sec θ = 1/cos θ, cot θ = 1/tan θ.

8 · Statistics

Definition • Importance • Data types • Mean, Median, Mode • Detailed methods • Quiz

8.1 Definition & Importance

Statistics is the art and science of collecting, organizing, presenting, analyzing and interpreting data to make decisions.

8.2 Types of data & methods of collection

  • Primary data: collected first-hand (surveys, experiments).
  • Secondary data: collected from existing sources (books, internet).
  • Qualitative (categorical) and quantitative (numerical) data.

8.3 Measures of central tendency — Mean, Median, Mode (deep theory)

Mean (arithmetic mean): sum of observations divided by number of observations. For n observations x₁..xₙ: mean = (Σxᵢ) / n.

Median: the middle value when observations are ordered. If n is even, median = average of the two middle values.

Mode: the most frequent observation(s). A set may be unimodal, bimodal or have no mode.

8.4 Calculations for grouped data (summary)

For grouped data, use class midpoints and frequencies to compute mean approximately: mean ≈ (Σf_i × m_i) / Σf_i, where f_i is class frequency and m_i is class midpoint.

Worked examples

  1. Compute mean of 2,4,6,8,10: mean = (2+4+6+8+10)/5 = 30/5 = 6.
  2. For data {1,2,2,3,3,4}, mode is {2,3} (bimodal), median is average of middle two = (2+3)/2 = 2.5.

9 · Probability

Basic definitions • Examples with coin & dice • Simple rules • Quiz

9.1 Basic definition

Probability measures the likelihood of an event. For equally likely outcomes: P(E) = favorable outcomes / total outcomes.

9.2 Sample space & events

Sample space (S) is the set of all possible outcomes. Example: for coin toss S = {H, T}. An event is any subset of S.

9.3 Worked examples

  1. Probability of head in coin toss = 1/2.
  2. Probability of getting 4 on fair die = 1/6.
  3. Probability of even number on die = 3/6 = 1/2.

10 · Linear Equations

One variable • Two variables • Methods • Graphs • Examples • Quiz

10.1 Linear equations in one variable

A linear equation in one variable has the form ax + b = 0. Solve by isolating x: x = −b/a when a≠0.

10.2 Linear equations in two variables

General form: ax + by = c. Solution is typically a pair (x,y). Methods of solution include substitution, elimination, and graphing.

10.3 Methods

  • Substitution: solve one equation for a variable and substitute.
  • Elimination: add/subtract equations to eliminate a variable.
  • Graphing: draw lines; intersection gives solution.

Worked example

Solve: 2x + 3 = 11. Subtract 3: 2x = 8 ⇒ x = 4.
